Methodology

The Problem with Career Averages

A player who averages 50 over 20 Tests is not the same as someone who averages 50 over 180 Tests. The second player sustained that level for nine times longer — through form slumps, injuries, pitch conditions across decades, and the wear of 160 extra matches. Career averages treat them identically. Our index does not.

Stint-Based Approach

We divide every career into 10-match windows and compute batting and bowling averages independently for each window. If the final leftover is shorter than 10 matches, it merges into the previous window (e.g., 71 matches → stints of 10, 10, 10, 10, 10, 10, 11). This gives us a picture of how a player performed at each stage of their career — not just one blended number.

Minimum Qualification

Each stint requires ≥ 10 batting innings (batting) or ≥ 10 bowling innings (bowling) to count. This filters out part-time players and prevents small samples from producing misleading averages.

How the Index Works

Think of it as the area under the curve of a player's stint scores plotted over their career. A player who bats well for 15 stints accumulates more area than someone equally skilled over 5 stints. This integral naturally rewards both quality and longevity.

Batting Excellence Index (BEI)

BEI = (∑ stint_bat_avg × stint_matches) / M0.7

The sum of each stint's batting average weighted by matches, divided by a career-length adjustment. Averaging 55 across fifteen 10-match stints produces a far higher BEI than averaging 55 across three stints.

Bowling Score Transformation

Bowling averages are "lower is better," so we flip them and factor in wickets per match:

bowl_score = (1000 ÷ bowl_avg) × √(wpm ÷ baseline_wpm)

This captures both quality (average) and volume (wickets per match). A bowler averaging 22 and taking 5 wickets per match scores far higher than one averaging 22 but taking only 1 per match. The square root dampens the volume factor so quality still dominates.

Bowling Excellence Index (BoEI)

BoEI = (∑ bowl_score × stint_matches) × scale / M0.7

Same integral concept as BEI, but for bowling scores. A data-driven scaling factor () ensures that the average bowling stint contributes equally to the average batting stint, so the two indices are comparable.

Why M0.7?

The raw integral grows with career length, so we divide by Mα (where α = 0.7) to control how much longevity matters. At α = 1.0, a 20-Test player averaging 50 and a 180-Test player averaging 50 would rate equally — career length wouldn't matter at all. At α = 0.5, the 180-Test player would rate 3× higher — longevity dominates. At α = 0.7, sustaining excellence over a long career is clearly rewarded, but quality per match still matters most.

Allrounder Excellence Index (AEI)

AEI = BEI + BoEI

Since BoEI already incorporates wickets per match, the straight sum naturally rewards players who contribute with both bat and ball. A part-time bowler who barely takes wickets will have a negligible BoEI regardless of their bowling average. A genuine allrounder must also achieve a minimum rating of 250 in both batting and bowling to qualify.

Era Adjustment

Not all eras are created equal. Averaging 50 in the 1950s (when pitches were uncovered and the overall Test average was ~30) is a far greater achievement than averaging 50 in the 2000s (when flat pitches pushed the overall average above 33). We normalize for this.

For each player, we look up the overall Test average (total runs ÷ total wickets) across all matches played during their career span. We then compare it to the all-time average ():

Batting factor = All-time avg ÷ Era avg
Bowling factor = Era avg ÷ All-time avg

A player from a low-scoring era (e.g., era avg = 28) gets a batting boost of ~1.14× and a bowling penalty of ~0.88×. A player from a high-scoring era (e.g., era avg = 34) gets a batting penalty of ~0.94× and a bowling boost of ~1.07×.

These factors are applied to BEI and BoEI before the rating conversion, so the final ratings reflect how impressive a player's performance was relative to the difficulty of their era.

Rating Scale (0–1000)

Era-adjusted indices are converted to an ICC-style rating using z-scores with square-root compression:

Rating = 350 + 351 × √z    where z = (value − μ) / σ

The square root compresses extreme outliers. Ratings above 900 represent all-time elite players, 800+ is great, and 700+ is very good. The median Test player sits around 350. Only Bradman (batting) and Kallis (allrounder) have broken 1000 — reflecting their status as the most extreme statistical outliers in Test cricket history.
