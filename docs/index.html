Methodology

The Problem with Career Averages

Traditional career averages are cumulative — they smooth over an entire career into a single number. This hides crucial information: how consistently did a player perform across different phases of their career? A player who averaged 50 for their first 30 tests then 25 for the next 70 looks the same as someone who averaged 32 throughout.

Stint-Based Approach

We divide every career into 10-match stints and compute batting and bowling averages independently for each stint. If the final leftover is shorter than 10 matches, it gets absorbed into the previous stint (e.g., 71 matches → stints of 10, 10, 10, 10, 10, 10, 11).

Minimum Qualification

Each stint requires ≥ 10 dismissals for the batting average to count, and ≥ 10 wickets for the bowling average to count. This filters out part-time batsmen/bowlers and naturally caps outlier stints from not-out inflation.

Batting Excellence Index (BEI)

BEI = (∑ stint_bat_avg × stint_matches) / M0.75

The integral of batting score over matches, adjusted for career length. Higher batting averages sustained over more matches produce a bigger area under the curve.

Bowling Score Transformation

Bowling averages are "lower is better," so we transform them: bowl_score = 1000 / bowl_avg. This maps bowling averages to a scale where higher is better: avg 20 → score 50, avg 25 → 40, avg 30 → 33.

Bowling Excellence Index (BoEI)

BoEI = (∑ bowl_score × stint_matches) × scale / M0.75

The raw BoEI is rescaled by a data-driven factor () so that the average qualifying bowling stint contributes equally to the average qualifying batting stint. This factor is computed from match-weighted means across all players.

Career-Length Adjustment (M0.75)

All indices are divided by Mα where α = 0.75. This means quality per match matters more than sheer longevity, but long careers are still rewarded. At α = 0.5 (√M), long careers dominate; at α = 1.0, it's purely per-match average and career length is irrelevant.

Allrounder Excellence Index (AEI)

AEI = BEI + BoEI

A genuine allrounder must have their weaker discipline contribute at least 25% of their total AEI. This filters out specialists — Tendulkar (BoEI ≈ 0) and Warne (BEI = 54) don't qualify despite high total scores.
